1.1. The Future of Higher Education

The Higher education existed in the Greek and Roman empires well before the modern universities came into existence in the Middle Ages (Haskins, 1965). Society has continued to value the contribution of higher education institutions in the centuries since. However, as societies have changed and developed, societal expectations of higher education institutions have not remained constant. The resilience of higher education, given the many challenges to it, including the rapid and wide-spread technological advancements in society is considered remarkable (Flavin, 2017). Higher education institutions have had to continually adapt to their changing environment based on its support for future innovation and productivity, and it is considered to be a strong predictor of a country’s economic prosperity (OECD, 2018), on the basis of its contribution to research and development (Zapp, 2017).

The world today is changing at an unprecedented pace, and the world is experiencing a paradigm shift worldwide with the digital transformation of higher education. It would perhaps be unwise to assume that COVID-19 will be the last global emergency of its kind, and that there will be no need for future generations to learn the lessons of the pandemic. A paradigm shift must take place in the global education system worldwide by narrowing the education-industry knowledge and skill gap. The focus has shifted from employment to employability; that is, educating students so they are capable of evolving and adapting to any changes that may arise. This is no small task but one that higher education is well-suited for. This will require input from students, faculty, employers and government authorities. Part of this paradigm shift entails shifting the focus from a competence-based learning model to a lifelong-lifewide learning model, which is critical in an intensely competitive and highly fluid knowledge-based economy and society (Blessinger et al., 2022). One of the major goals of any higher education institution is to make their students employable so that they can successfully survive the labour market of the future. However, this requires that higher education institutions focus on preparing students for continuous learning (that is, lifelong-lifewide learning). The soft skills required to survive in the labour market of the future include resilience, critical thinking, creative thinking, green skills, while OECD, 2017 agreed that in addition, problem-solving, and social and emotional skills, such as teamwork, communication and cultural awareness are also essential among others. To this end, the curricula should be updated to reflect this reality.

Higher education today is viewed as the prime educational mechanism for creating work-ready graduates for a knowledge-based society (Nerland et al., 2018). The success of a higher education institution and its teaching is being assessed by observers in terms of graduates’ employability in society (Osborne and Grant-Smith, 2017). Despite the many potential benefits associated with completing a higher education degree, for most students their motivation to complete a higher education degree is based on the belief of greater employment opportunities (Norton et al., 2019). The institutions are being transformed to focus on satisfying student and employer needs, and many programmes are becoming more skill-based in orientation to provide appropriate competencies for future professions (Fuchs and Henning, 2017; Milburn-Shaw and Walker, 2017). The use of digital technologies is being mandated by higher education institutions to allow teachers to enhance their teaching practices and to ensure students are equipped with knowledge, skills, and dispositions for working in the 21st century (Nykvist and Mukherjee, 2016).

The power of education is to bring about profound change.The pandemic has only served to prove our fragility and our interconnectedness. Now urgent action, taken together, is needed to change course and reimagine our futures. UNESCO (2021) agreed that a critical step towards reimagining our futures is to forge a new social contract (an implicit agreement among members of a society to cooperate for shared benefit) for education that aims to rebuild our relationships with each other, with the planet, and with technology, and allow us to think differently about learning and the relationships between students, teachers, knowledge, and the world and that will help us build peaceful, just, and sustainable futures for all.
